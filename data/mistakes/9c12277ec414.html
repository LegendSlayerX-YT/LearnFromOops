Problem

5 Is the sequence $\{(\frac{1}{3})^{n+1}\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ bounded? A. Upper bounded B. Lower bounded C. Both D. Neither

Student Answer

C. Both

Correct Answer

C. Both

How to Solve

A sequence is called bounded if it has both an upper bound and a lower bound. 1. Let's look at the terms of the sequence $a_n = (\frac{1}{3})^{n+1}$ starting from $n=1$. 2. The first term is $a_1 = (\frac{1}{3})^{1+1} = \frac{1}{3^2} = \frac{1}{9}$. 3. The second term is $a_2 = (\frac{1}{3})^{2+1} = \frac{1}{3^3} = \frac{1}{27}$. 4. As $n$ increases, the terms continue to get smaller and smaller, approaching $0$ as a limit. 5. Since all terms in the sequence are positive, the sequence is lower bounded by $0$. 6. Since the terms are always decreasing, the sequence is upper bounded by its very first term, $\frac{1}{9}$. 7. Because the sequence has both a lower bound and an upper bound, it is bounded. Thus, the correct choice is C.
